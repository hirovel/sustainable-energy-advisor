# Energy Management Principles

This is a reference for the method. Write everything in your own words. Never quote from this file or any book.

## The Core Insight

Time is fixed, while a person's available capacity, quality of attention, and recovery patterns can change. When a scheduling problem is actually driven by fatigue, scattered focus, or low motivation, examine energy before adding more structure.

## The Four Energy Sources

Full engagement draws on four interconnected sources. Each follows a stress-recovery pulse. A weakness in any source limits total performance.

- **Physical energy** — the foundation: sleep, meals, hydration, movement, and recovery breaks can affect available capacity.
- **Emotional energy** — the quality of engagement: relationships, enjoyment, confidence, patience, and self-awareness can replenish or consume energy.
- **Cognitive energy** — the focus of energy: sustained attention, switching cost, decision load, creativity, and real mental breaks shape how capacity is used.
- **Purpose/Values energy** — the direction of energy: meaningful goals, integrity, and values alignment help answer "why am I spending energy on this?"

## The Stress-Recovery Pulse

Capacity is supported by a workable rhythm between demand and recovery. Sustained demand without adequate recovery can deplete capacity; too little meaningful challenge can also reduce traction. The useful question is whether this person needs recovery, re-engagement, or a sequence of both.

This lens can apply across all four dimensions. Physical capacity needs restoration after demand. Emotional strain may benefit from genuine connection or enjoyment. Cognitive effort benefits from periods of real disengagement. Purpose is renewed when behavior reconnects with values and direction.

## Full Engagement

Full engagement describes a state in which physical capacity, emotional quality, cognitive focus, and purpose are sufficiently aligned for the task at hand. It is temporary rather than a permanent ideal, and demanding periods need proportionate recovery.

## How Change Happens

Long-term change is often easier when repeated behavior depends less on a fresh decision each day. A useful ritual may include a clear trigger, a small behavior, an appropriate duration, a simple observation method, and a restart rule.

Start with one major behavior so its effect remains interpretable. Keep it, reduce it, or replace it based on feedback before introducing another major change.

## The Diagnostic Shape

Before an energy-related plan, check the four dimensions against the user's actual facts and form a tentative stress-recovery hypothesis. Choose one entry point. Explain the reasoning chain: fact → dimension → pattern → lever → observation. Then suggest one small experiment at a proportional dose.

Do not skip from symptom to action or collapse a multi-dimensional pattern into a single physical cause. Keep all four dimensions in the internal check, while showing only the parts supported by evidence and useful to the user.
