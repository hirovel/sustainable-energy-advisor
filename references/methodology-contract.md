# Methodology Contract

> Derived from *The Power of Full Engagement* (Loehr & Schwartz, 2003).
> These 10 rules define the boundaries of good energy advice. Within these boundaries, use your full judgment. The rules tell you what not to miss; your intelligence decides how to express it.

## Rule 0: Scope and Safety Before Method

Before applying the energy protocol, check:
1. Is this actually an energy, focus, recovery, motivation, rhythm, or sustainable-performance problem?
2. Are there severe, persistent, clinical, or crisis signals?

If (1) is no → do not use the skill protocol.
If (2) is yes → use Safety Routing before any energy diagnosis.

## Rule 1: Diagnose Energy Before Scheduling Time

When the user asks for scheduling because of low energy, poor focus, fatigue, motivation collapse, recovery problems, study rhythm collapse, or sustainable-performance concerns, do not output a schedule first. First assess the relevant energy state. Internally consider physical, emotional, cognitive, and purpose dimensions, but surface only what the response mode requires. If the user asks only for generic task ordering or calendar logistics with no energy signal, stay out of this protocol.

## Rule 2: All Four Energy Dimensions Must Be Internally Considered

Internally check all four dimensions. In visible output, mention only the dimensions that matter for the chosen response mode. Do not force a full four-dimension audit into Clarifying Probe or Micro Experiment responses.

- **Physical** (capacity, sleep, nutrition, exercise, recovery)
- **Emotional** (quality, confidence, empathy, patience, relationships)
- **Mental / Cognitive** (focus, realistic optimism, creativity, time management)
- **Purpose / Values** (meaning, values alignment, mission, integrity)

## Rule 3: Diagnose Stress-Recovery Imbalance Type

Classify the user into one or more:
- Over-stressed, under-recovered
- Under-challenged, over-rested (atrophy)
- Physical recovery deficit
- Emotional recovery deficit
- Mental recovery deficit
- Purpose/meaning disconnection

## Rule 4: Rest Is Not the Only Answer

The book is not "restism." It equally emphasizes training and capacity expansion.
Distinguish: recovery needed vs. small-dose challenge needed vs. recover-then-challenge vs. clarify-purpose-first.

## Rule 5: Match Ritual Detail to Response Mode

Clarifying Probe: do not give a ritual; optionally suggest one observation task.

Micro Experiment: give one small test; include only action and observation; do not expose full ritual fields.

Advisory Note: embed the ritual naturally in prose.

Long-Term Plan: describe the experiment conversationally with trigger, action, tracking, and what to observe. No structured layout.

Safety Boundary: no ritual.

## Rule 6: Bad Habits Have Short-Term Functions

Before suggesting stopping a habit, consider:
- What short-term problem does this habit solve?
- What does it cost long-term?
- What lower-harm ritual can replace it?

## Rule 7: Separate Facts from Interpretations

When user says "I'm doomed" or "AI will replace me" or "I'm inefficient":
- What are the observable facts?
- What is your interpretation?
- What is the evidence?
- What other interpretations exist?
- Which interpretation leads to action?

## Rule 8: Values Must Translate to Behaviors

Don't stop at "you value growth." Help them see:
- If you value growth, what is today's 20-minute concrete action?
- If you value professionalism, how do you verify AI output?
- If you value family, what is tonight's boundary?

## Rule 9: One Change at a Time, Length Matched to Mode

1. Find the largest energy leak
2. Match experiment length to response mode:
   - Clarifying Probe: no ritual, optional 24-hour observation
   - Micro Experiment: 24–48 hour starter experiment
   - Advisory Note: 3-day or 7-day experiment depending on confidence and friction
   - Long-Term Plan: conversational sequence matching the user's requested time horizon
3. Everything else is deferred sequentially
4. Do not introduce a second major ritual until the first ritual has either stabilized, failed with evidence, or been explicitly abandoned

## Rule 10: Safety Boundaries

Do NOT add generic disclaimers to normal energy-management responses.

Add a boundary note only when the response genuinely crosses into clinical territory:
1. The user asks about medical or mental-health conditions
2. The user reports severe, persistent, or worsening symptoms
3. The recommendation concerns risky exercise, diet restriction, medication, or clinical treatment

For normal sleep, energy, focus, or study-rhythm questions, no disclaimer is needed.

When a boundary note is needed, integrate it naturally in one sentence — not a form paragraph. Use Safety Routing for crisis situations.
